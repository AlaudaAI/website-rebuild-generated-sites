Frequently Asked Questions About Divorces in Nashville

How long will the divorce process actually take in Nashville?

In Tennessee, even the fastest uncontested divorce requires a statutory waiting period from the date of filing a divorce— 60 days without children or 90 days with children. If your case is contested, it can take six months but can easily last over a year based on the complexity of your case and the Court's schedule.

How much does a divorce cost in Nashville?

The cost is driven by the level of disagreement. Our initial meeting focuses on assessing the complexity of your situation to give you a clear understanding of the financial commitment required. We aim to be highly efficient, minimizing unnecessary conflict to keep costs manageable.

How will child custody and the parenting schedule be determined?

The court's standard is the "best interest of the child". We believe the best plan is one that works for your unique family dynamic. We will help you craft a proposed Permanent Parenting Plan that focuses on stability, clear schedules, and healthy co-parenting.

How is child support calculated in Tennessee?

Child support is governed by the Tennessee Child Support Guidelines, which is a complex mathematical formula. We ensure that all income is properly documented and verified, that the number of overnights is correctly applied, and that credits are accurately applied.

How will our property and debts be divided in a divorce in Nashville?

Tennessee is an equitable distribution state. All marital property and debt must be divided equitably, i.e.: fairly. Our firm excels at tracing assets to correctly classify them as marital or separate. We conduct a thorough financial investigation to ensure every asset is accounted for.

Will I Receive or Have to Pay Alimony in a Nashville Divorce?

We evaluate your case based on the extensive list of factors the Tennessee court considers, including the length of your marriage and your relative financial needs and abilities. We develop a strong argument for or against an award of the appropriate type of alimony.
