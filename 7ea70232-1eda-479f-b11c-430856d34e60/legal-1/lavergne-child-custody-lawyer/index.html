An Overview of Child Custody in Tennessee

There were over 13 million custodial parents throughout the United States, emphasizing just how common child custody matters are across the country. In Tennessee, family courts are required to prioritize the best interests of the child when making any custody orders. When creating a custody order, a family judge will divide the following types of custody rights:

  1. Physical Custody. This dictates which parent has the right to have the child live with or visit them. It also requires the parent with physical custody to provide for a child's basic needs, including giving them food, shelter, and clothes.
  2. Legal Custody. This form of custody dictates who gets to make important decisions for the child, like the type of medical care they receive or where they attend school.

How Child Custody Is Divided in Tennessee

When making custody decisions, a family judge will either award sole or joint custody in La Vergne. Sole custody would mean only one parent has physical or legal custody rights, while joint custody allows parents to split these rights. In a sole custody situation, the noncustodial parent can still participate in co-parenting with the custodial parent through visitation.

Because children tend to fare better with both parents in their lives, family courts in La Vergne aim to create joint custody agreements whenever possible. However, if there are extenuating circumstances or if one parent is unsafe for the child, then other arrangements will likely be made. What Should You Avoid Doing in a Child Custody Case in Tennessee?

When you're involved in a child custody case, especially a contentious one, it's important to avoid doing anything that may make a family court rule against you. Having outbursts, using foul language, talking poorly about the other parent, or putting your own needs above your child can all have a negative impact on your child custody case. Other mistakes include violating a court order or withholding parenting time from the other parent.

Is It Hard to Get Sole Custody in Tennessee?

Securing sole custody in Tennessee can be difficult, as courts work to prioritize the best interests of children by working toward joint custody agreements whenever possible. However, if you can prove the other parent is incapable of caring for your child, cannot make sound decisions on your child's behalf, or is a danger to your child, you may be able to secure sole legal and physical custody in Tennessee.

Does Every Child Custody Case Go to Court in Tennessee?

Not every child custody case must go through litigation in local courts in Tennessee. If two parents can make an amicable, fair agreement on child custody matters outside of court, they can submit that agreement to the court for approval and avoid litigation. If a couple cannot agree on their own custody terms, which is the most common scenario in child custody cases, then they must move proceedings to their local court, where a judge will make the final decision.
