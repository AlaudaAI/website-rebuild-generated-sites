Understanding Tennessee's Child Support Guidelines

Our state's child support guidelines are intended to account for a wide variety of circumstances, but given the diversity among families and their situations, the judge has relatively limited discretion to deviate from these guidelines.

Tennessee follows the income shares model, which means that the amount of child support a parent will be ordered to pay is calculated based on both parents' total earnings and income. Though there are exceptions, most parents and attorneys use the state's child support calculator and worksheet to determine payments. These tools take into account each parent's income, the number of children requiring support, and how often each parent has the children.

The following factors influence child support calculations in Murfreesboro and across Tennessee:

  • The quality of life the child is used to: The court aims to maintain a child's existing standard of living as closely as possible.
  • History of family violence: If there's a documented history of family violence, this may affect the support arrangement.
  • Special needs or medical considerations: The court considers any special educational or medical needs the child might have, as well as any needs or disabilities associated with either parent.

The guidelines cover basic care but may not include all expenses required to maintain the child's accustomed lifestyle. These costs can include out-of-pocket expenses, extracurricular activities, or private school tuition. A child support lawyer in Murfreesboro can present a thorough financial snapshot to the court so that decisions are grounded in accurate and complete information.

Consequences of Unpaid Child Support in Tennessee

In Tennessee, no set number of missed child support payments will result in jail time. However, missing three months of payments in a row may increase the risk of jail time.

If a parent is more than $2,500 behind in child support payments, the court may find them in contempt and sentence them to jail.

Additionally, parents who willfully refuse to pay child support may face criminal charges and possible jail time.

Unpaid child support can lead to serious financial repercussions beyond legal penalties. For example:

  • Wage garnishments: The state can take money directly from paychecks.
  • Tax refund interceptions: The government may seize tax returns to recover past due support.
  • Liens on property: The court can place a hold on assets or property to secure payment.
  • Credit score impact: Falling behind on support could lower your credit rating and make it harder to secure loans or employment.

Legal guidance can help you navigate these consequences and seek practical resolutions.

How Long Do You Go to Jail for Child Support in TN?

In Tennessee, non-support is considered a class A misdemeanor, which can carry a jail sentence of up to 6 months for non-payment. In severe support cases, a parent might receive criminal charges for not paying court-ordered child support. This Class E felony charge—known as criminal arrest for flagrant non-support—can carry a sentence of 1 to 6 years in prison.

If the court sees a valid reason for missed payments, such as job loss or medical issues, jail time becomes less likely.

The court may also order the noncustodial parent's wages to be garnished, their driver's license suspended, or their passport denied.

Addressing root causes of payment delays, like major financial changes, and seeking a modification of the order when needed, can reduce the risk of severe penalties. What Is the Process for Modifying Child Support in Tennessee?

Modifying a child support order in Tennessee requires a significant change in circumstances. Common reasons include:

  • Changes in income: A reduction or increase in your income, or your co-parent's income.
  • Job loss: Losing employment or a major reduction in hours.
  • Change in child's needs: New expenses for education or medical care.

To seek a modification, file a petition with the court and provide evidence of your changed circumstances. The court will review your evidence and, if the change is substantial (usually a 15% variance in income), may adjust the child support order. A child support attorney Murfreesboro families trust can explain the process and help you gather and present all necessary information to the court.

How Is Parenting Time Considered in Child Support Calculations?

Parenting time can significantly impact child support calculations in Tennessee. The state uses the income shares model, which considers how many overnights a child spends with each parent each year. Generally, the more overnight time a non-custodial parent spends with a child, the less child support they might need to pay as they directly support the child on those days. Accurate records of overnight stays and a clear parenting schedule ensure a fair calculation.
