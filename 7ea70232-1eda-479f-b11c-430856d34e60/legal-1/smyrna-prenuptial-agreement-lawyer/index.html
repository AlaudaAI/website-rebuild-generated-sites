What Is a Prenuptial Agreement?

A prenuptial agreement is a contract two people enter into before they marry. It outlines the rights and responsibilities of each person, along with the assets each party retains in the event of a divorce or separation. Despite their negative reputation, prenuptial agreements are great tools to protect couples if their marriage fails, but it certainly isn't an indicator that a split is inevitable. In fact, only 15% of married couples had prenups in 2022, up from 3% in 2010. Compare that to the divorce rate in the U.S., which has fallen relatively steadily since 2012, and it's hard to argue that prenuptial agreements mean divorce is likely. Regardless, the reputation these documents have is unwarranted, and you shouldn't be afraid to ask your partner to sign one with you.

Benefits of a Prenup

Prenuptial agreements are customizable to a couple's needs, making sure each partner receives fair treatment in the creation process. These agreements:

  • Provide financial transparency going into the marriage.
  • Protect premarital assets.
  • Outline debt responsibilities.
  • Make the divorce process easier, should it become necessary.

Similarly, you can include terms about spousal support arrangements in a prenup.

Reasons to Challenge a Prenuptial Agreement

While prenuptial agreements are legally binding, there may be circumstances in which you can challenge the document and have it deemed unenforceable. Some common reasons to dispute a prenup include the following:

  • It was entered into under duress or coercion. If one party is threatened, pressured, or forced into signing the document, it is not legally enforceable.
  • One party committed fraud or misrepresented facts. Both parties must provide a full picture of their assets, income, and debt. Lying or omitting information with the intent to defraud or trick the other party can make the deal void.
  • The document is too one-sided. If the terms clearly favor one party over the other, it is inherently unfair. In that situation, you can argue that the agreement cannot be enforced.
  • The agreement includes illegal clauses. The prenup cannot subject either party to illegal terms, such as dictating expected behaviors during the marriage.

Keep in mind that the agreement must be in writing, signed by both parties, and notarized to be enforceable. Challenging a prenuptial agreement in the Superior Court of Cobb County is not easy, but you have a stronger chance of success with an experienced and knowledgeable attorney by your side.

FAQs

What Type of Lawyer Is Ideal for a Prenup?

The ideal type of lawyer for a prenup is one with experience in family law and marriage law. Prenup laws can be complicated, and you want an attorney who has the knowledge and experience to successfully manage your case and make sure the terms of your agreement are fair for everyone. It's wise for each partner to have their own attorney to promote the spirit of fairness in drafting the agreement.

Can I Write My Own Prenup in Smyrna, TN?

Yes, you can write your own prenup in Smyrna, TN, but that's probably not a great idea. If you don't have experience in drafting these agreements, you may end up creating a document that isn't actually enforceable. To enter into the agreement, the parties must have full knowledge of their assets, and their signing must be free of coercion. Also, you may end up omitting important provisions or limiting the assets you'll have rights to in the event of a divorce.

Are Prenups Enforceable in Tennessee?

Yes, prenups are enforceable in Tennessee, assuming the agreement was entered into by both parties freely, without duress, and with full disclosure of the assets involved. The agreement must be in writing, and both parties must sign it. If you need to challenge a prenup, contact a Smyrna prenuptial agreement attorney. They can review the document to see if it's legally enforceable before challenging it in court.

How Much Money Justifies a Prenup?

There's no specific dollar amount that justifies a prenup. While these documents have a reputation for only being reserved for wealthy individuals, prenuptial agreements are great legal tools for any couple to protect themselves from liability. Prenups are generally recommended for people with significant assets or who own businesses. These agreements also protect partners from each other's premarital debt.
